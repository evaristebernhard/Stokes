Introduction
Consider algebraic surfaces in complex projective threespace P 3 , denote by a node of such a
surface an ordinary double point and by µ (d) the maximal number of nodes of an algebraic
surface of degree d in P 3 with no further degeneracies. This note shows that µ (8) ≥ 168
by giving an example of an octic surface X8 with 168 nodes. X8 is found within a seven
parameter family of 112–nodal octic surfaces admitting dihedral symmetry of order sixteen.
This improves the estimate of µ (8) given by examples of Gallarati ([G], µ (8) ≥ 160) and Kreiss
([K], µ (8) ≥ 160). On the other hand, using Miyaokas upper bound [Mi] for the number of
nodes of a projective surface, we get µ (8) ≤ 174, thus
168 ≤ µ (8) ≤ 174.
I have made excessive use of the computer algebra system Maple V R3 computing X8 , in
particular the calculations depend heavily on some new resp. improved features of release 3
([Ma], pp. 22–24). The construction of X8 involves no free parameters, and in fact D. van
Straten calculated that X8 is rigid using MACAULAY.
The octic X8 is invariant under the group D8 × Z2 , therefore invariant under the reflection
group Z32 of order eight. So X8 is the eightfold cover of a projective quartic surface with thirteen
nodes. This construction is referred to as the Segre–trick and it has been used extensively to
construct sextic surfaces with a given number of nodes, see [C] and [B].
From the existence of X8 one however cannot deduce the existence of surfaces with 161 up
to 167 nodes. It can be checked that the family of surfaces X8 is constructed from contains no
such surfaces.
Acknowledgments: I would like to thank W. Barth for suggesting to me that surfaces with many
nodes can be found among Dn –symmetric surfaces.

The first step is to set c = f = h = 0, then X is D8 × Z2 –symmetric. Now both C0 and C1
admit reflection symmetry Z22 and therefore can be constructed from plane quartic curves C̃0
and C̃1 by applying the Segre–trick. The second step is to set e = −1 to mod out all projective
transformations z 7→ λ · z, λ ∈ C∗ from this family of octics.
Now call a singularity of C̃0 or C̃1 outside of the coordinate axes a singularity in general
position. Analogously call a point of contact of C̃0 or C̃1 to one of the coordinate axes outside
the three points of intersection of those axes a point of contact in general position. Applying
proposition 5 of [C] gives:
• C̃0 has got two nodes s1 and s2 and two contact points t1 and t2 to {w = 0} in general
position.
• C̃1 has got four nodes u1 , . . . , u4 in general position, no three of them collinear and thus
splits into two conics.
• Every node in general position of C̃0 or C̃1 induces an orbit of 16 singularities on X.
• Every point of contact in general position of either C̃0 or C̃1 to one of the coordinate axes
{w = 0} or {z = 0} induces an orbit of 8 singularities on X.

X8 is smooth away from the 168 nodes
Now one checks (again with Maple) that the 168 nodes are the only ones to appear on the eight
planes Ej , j = 0, . . . , 7:
• points of contact to coordinate axes can be computed explicitly,
• the fact that K is non degenerate can be checked by computing its determinant,

line not containing this node and computing the branch points with multiplicities.
One also checks that the points of intersection of X8 with the line L = {(x : y : 0 : 0) | (x : y) ∈ P 1 }
are smooth. If X8 would have a singularity p0 outside of all eight planes Ej , j = 0, . . . , 7 and
outside the line L, then p0 would induce an orbit of sixteen singularities of X8 . Now consider
the following lemma:
